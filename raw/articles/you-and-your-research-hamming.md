# You and Your Research

**Author:** Dr. Richard W. Hamming (Turing Award Winner)
**Occasion:** Bell Communications Research Colloquium Seminar, March 7, 1986
**Transcriber:** J. F. Kaiser, Bell Communications Research

---

## Core Question

**"Why do so few scientists make significant contributions and so many are forgotten in the long run?"**

---

## 1. Luck vs. Preparation

> "I find that the major objection is that people think great science is done by luck. It's all a matter of luck."

Consider Einstein. Note how many different things he did that were good. Was it all luck?
Consider Shannon. He didn't do just information theory. Several years before, he did some other good things and some which are still locked up in the security of cryptography.

**Pasteur:** "Luck favors the prepared mind."

> "The prepared mind sooner or later finds something important and does it. So yes, it is luck. The particular thing you do is luck, but that you do something is not."

> **Newton:** "If others would think as hard as I did, then they would get similar results."

> "When I came to Bell Labs, I shared an office for a while with Shannon. At the same time he was doing information theory, I was doing coding theory. It is suspicious that the two of us did it at the same place and at the same time — it was in the atmosphere. And you can say, 'Yes, it was luck.' On the other hand you can say, 'But why of all the people in Bell Labs then were those the two who did it?' Yes, it is partly luck, and partly it is the prepared mind."

---

## 2. What You Already Know vs. What You Don't

> "The great discovery, the great breakthrough — it is not in coming up with something; it is in recognizing something that was there all the time. The breakthrough is seeing what everyone has seen and thinking what no one has thought."

> "The real breakthrough is in recognizing that what everybody knows is sometimes wrong. That is the moment of insight."

---

## 3. Courage

One of the characteristics you see in great scientists is **courage**.

> "Shannon had tremendous courage. You have only to think of his major theorem. He wants to create a method of coding, but he doesn't know what to do so he makes a random code. Then he is stuck. And then he asks the impossible question, 'What would the average random code do?' He then proves that the average code is arbitrarily good, and that therefore there must be at least one good code. **Who but a man of infinite courage could have dared to think those thoughts?** That is the characteristic of great scientists; they have courage. They will go forward under incredible circumstances; they think and continue to think."

---

## 4. Age

> "Einstein did things very early, and all the quantum mechanic fellows were disgustingly young when they did their best work. Most mathematicians, theoretical physicists, and astrophysicists do what we consider their best work when they are young."

**Why age affects scientists:** Once you're famous, you find yourself on all kinds of committees and unable to do any more work.

> "When you are famous it is hard to work on small problems. This is what did Shannon in. After information theory, what do you do for an encore? The great scientists often make this error. They fail to continue to plant the little acorns from which the mighty oak trees grow."

> "The Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, in my opinion, has **ruined more good scientists** than any institution has created, judged by what they did before they came and judged by what they did after. Not that they weren't good afterwards, but they were superb before they got there and were only good afterwards."

---

## 5. Working Conditions

> "Very clearly they are not [the best] because people are often most productive when working conditions are bad. One of the better times of the Cambridge Physical Laboratories was when they had practically shacks — they did some of the best physics ever."

**Hamming's own story:** Early on he realized Bell Labs wasn't going to give him the conventional acre of programming people. He asked himself: "Why can't you make them write programs?" What appeared at first as a defect forced him into automatic programming very early.

> "What appears to be a fault, often, by a change of viewpoint, turns out to be one of the greatest assets you can have."

---

## 6. Drive — Compound Interest

> "Knowledge and productivity are like **compound interest**. Given two people of approximately the same ability and one person who works ten percent more than the other, the latter will more than twice outproduce the former. The more you know, the more you learn; the more you learn, the more you can do; the more you can do, the more the opportunity — it is very much like compound interest."

> "I don't want to give you a rate, but it is a very high rate. Given two people with exactly the same ability, the one person who manages day in and day out to get in one more hour of thinking will be tremendously more productive over a lifetime."

**Edison:** "Genius is 99% perspiration and 1% inspiration."

> "The steady application of effort with a little bit more work, intelligently applied is what gets you surprisingly far."

---

## 7. Ambiguity Tolerance

> "Most people like to believe something is or is not true. **Great scientists tolerate ambiguity very well.** They believe the theory enough to go ahead; they doubt it enough to notice the errors and faults so they can step forward and create the new replacement theory. If you believe too much you'll never notice the flaws; if you doubt too much you won't get started. It requires a lovely balance."

> "Most great scientists are well aware of why their theories are true and they are also well aware of some slight misfits which don't quite fit and they don't forget it."

**Darwin's practice:** He found it necessary to write down every piece of evidence which appeared to contradict his beliefs because otherwise they would disappear from his mind.

---

## 8. Emotional Commitment

> "Great contributions are rarely done by adding another decimal place. It comes down to **an emotional commitment.** Most great scientists are completely committed to their problem. Those who don't become committed seldom produce outstanding, first-class work."

> "Everybody who has studied creativity is driven finally to saying, 'creativity comes out of your subconscious.' Somehow, suddenly, there it is. It just appears. Well, we know very little about the subconscious."

> "Your subconscious has nothing to do but work on your problem if you are deeply immersed and committed to a topic, day after day after day. So you wake up one morning, or on some afternoon, and there's the answer. For those who don't get committed to their current problem, the subconscious goofs off on other things and doesn't produce the big result."

---

## 9. The Dining Table Method — Seek Out Important People

Hamming deliberately changed eating tables to find the most productive conversations:
- **Mathematicians:** learned very little
- **Physics table** (Shockley, Brattain, Bardeen, J.B. Johnson): learned a lot, but after the Nobel Prize "there was no use eating with them!"
- **Chemistry table:** asked Dave McCall "What are the important problems of your field?" Then: "If what you are doing is not important, and if you don't think it is going to lead to something important, why are you at Bell Labs working on it?"

Result: Dave McCall later became head of the department, Member of the National Academy of Engineering. The others at that table: never heard their names mentioned again.

---

## 10. Work on Important Problems

> "If you do not work on an important problem, it's unlikely you'll do important work. It's perfectly obvious."

**What makes a problem important?** Not its consequences — it is that you have a reasonable attack. That is what makes a problem important.

> "The three outstanding problems in physics, in a certain sense, were never worked on while I was at Bell Labs. By important I mean guaranteed a Nobel Prize. We didn't work on (1) time travel, (2) teleportation, and (3) antigravity. They are not important problems because we do not have an attack."

> "I speak Friday afternoons for years — **Great Thoughts Time** — I committed 10% of my time trying to understand the bigger problems in the field, i.e. what was and what was not important."

> "The great scientists, when an opportunity opens up, get after it and they pursue it. They drop all other things. They get rid of other things and they get after an idea because they had already thought the thing through. Their minds are prepared; they see the opportunity and they go after it."

---

## 11. The Open Door

> "I notice that if you have the door to your office closed, you get more work done today and tomorrow, and you are more productive than most. But 10 years later somehow you don't know quite know what problems are worth working on; all the hard work you do is sort of tangential in importance."

> "He who works with the door open gets all kinds of interruptions, but he also occasionally gets clues as to what the world is and what might be important."

> "There is a pretty good correlation between those who work with the doors open and those who ultimately do important things, although people who work with doors closed often work harder. Somehow they seem to work on slightly the wrong thing — not much, but enough that they miss fame."

---

## 12. Change the Problem — "It Ain't What You Do, It's the Way That You Do It"

Hamming's story: He was conned into doing a military integration job on a digital computer. Rather than just get the answer, he asked: "How do I demonstrate that digital can beat analog?" He created an elegant theory — "Hamming's Method of Integrating Differential Equations."

> "By changing the problem slightly, I did important work rather than trivial work."

> "Instead of attacking isolated problems, I made the resolution that I would never again solve an isolated problem except as characteristic of a class."

> "The effort to generalize often means that the solution is simple... The business of abstraction frequently makes things simple."

---

## 13. Selling Your Work

> "You have to learn to write clearly and well so that people will read it, you must learn to give reasonably formal talks, and you also must learn to give informal talks."

> "When I first started, I got practically physically ill while giving a speech, and I was very, very nervous. I realized I either had to learn to give speeches smoothly or I would essentially partially cripple my whole career."

> "The technical person wants to give a highly limited technical talk. Most of the time the audience wants a broad general talk and wants much more survey and background than the speaker is willing to give."

**How to give a talk:** Paint a general picture to say why it's important, then slowly give a sketch of what was done. Then people will say, "Yes, Mary really gave a good talk; I understand what Mary has done."

---

## 14. Why Capable People Fail — Personality Defects

**Defect 1: Total control.** A fellow wouldn't let the secretary help with anything. He never advanced beyond what he could do single-handedly.

> "You have to learn to work with the system and take advantage of all the system has to offer."

**Defect 2: Ego assertion.** Hamming had to decide: western clothes/bolo or conformity? He chose to "appear to conform" and got much better service.

> "I didn't say you should conform; I said **'The appearance of conforming gets you a long way.'** If you assert your ego in any number of ways, 'I am going to do it my way,' you pay a small steady price throughout the whole of your professional career."

**Defect 3: Fighting the system.**

> "Which do you want to be? The person who changes the system or the person who does first-class science? **My advice is to let somebody else do it and you get on with becoming a first-class scientist.** Very few of you have the ability to both reform the system and become a first-class scientist."

**Defect 4: Anger.** "Often a scientist becomes angry, and this is no way to handle things. Amusement, yes; anger, no."

---

## 15. Self-Delusion — The Alibi Trap

> "When you ask, 'Why didn't you do such and such,' the person has a thousand alibis. If you look at the history of science, usually these days there are 10 people right there ready, and we pay off for the person who is there first. The other nine fellows say, 'Well, I had the idea but I didn't do it.'"

> "**Don't try an alibi. Don't try and kid yourself.** You can tell other people all the alibis you want. But to yourself try to be honest."

---

## 16. Is It Worth It?

> "Doing really first-class work, and knowing it, is as good as wine, women and song put together."

> "The value is in the struggle more than it is in the result. The struggle to make something of yourself seems to be worthwhile in itself. The success and fame are sort of dividends, in my opinion."

---

## Q&A Highlights

**On stress:** "If you want to be a great scientist you're going to have to put up with stress. You can lead a nice life; you can be a nice guy or you can be a great scientist. But **nice guys end last.**"

**On brainstorming:** "I find it desirable to talk to other people; but a session of brainstorming is seldom worthwhile... You want to get rid of those sound absorbers who are nice people but merely say, 'Oh yes,' and find those who will stimulate you right back."

**On reading too much:** "There was a fellow at Bell Labs, a very, very smart guy. He was always in the library; he read everything... If you read all the time what other people have done you will think the way they thought."

**On UNIX origin:** "Give them the smallest machine you can because they are very able people. They will learn how to do things on a small machine instead of mass computing. As far as I'm concerned, that's how UNIX arose."

---

## Final Words

> "I claim that some of the reasons why so many people who have greatness within their grasp don't succeed are:
> - they don't work on important problems
> - they don't become emotionally involved
> - they don't try and change what is difficult to some other situation
> - they keep giving themselves alibis why they don't

> **Therefore, go forth and become great scientists!**"
